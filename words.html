Can a policy be too credible?
The U.S. wants to slow China's AI. So it reaches for compute, the chips, clusters & clouds that train frontier models.

But China can't read Washington's mind. It only sees what the U.S. is willing to pay for, and has to guess: is this real resolve… or just talk?
This is a signaling game. You'll play the U.S., send a costly signal, and watch China update its beliefs, sometimes in ways you didn't intend.
let's play →
Two players, one downstream audience
The U.S. is the sender.
Secretly High or Low Resolve. It chooses a compute policy and pays its cost.

China is the receiver.
It cannot see U.S. resolve. It updates belief μ(H|s), then adapts or waits.
Vietnam is the downstream audience.
It does not choose the U.S. signal or China's response. But it observes the fallout, updates its constraints, and looks for room to maneuver.

The twist: credibility ≠ control.
Nature chooses U.S. type → U.S. sends signal → China updates μ(H|s) → China adapts or waits → Vietnam adjusts downstream
A signal becomes credible when a Low-Resolve U.S. would not bother paying for it. That is what makes cost informative.

Sunk costs
Paid upfront and not recovered, whatever the receiver does.
Reducible costs
Paid upfront, but partly offset if the sender follows through.
Installment costs
Future costs paid over time, whatever the receiver does.
Tied-hands costs
Future costs paid only if the sender backs down.
Export controls look sunk. Thresholds can be reducible. Provider oversight gains force through installments.

The math, if you want it
China updates with Bayes' rule:
μ(H|s) = Pr(s|H)·p ⁄ [ Pr(s|H)·p + Pr(s|L)·(1−p) ]
China adapts once belief clears its threshold:
μ(H|s) ≥ μ* = (q + l) ⁄ (g + d + l)
q = cost of adapting · g = gain if the U.S. is High · d = danger of waiting · l = option value of waiting.
The paradox: even a perfectly credible signal (μ→1) can make adapting the smart move, so credibility can accelerate the very substitution it tried to prevent.
