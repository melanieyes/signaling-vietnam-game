Can a policy be too credible?
The U.S. wants to slow China's AI. So it reaches for compute, the chips, clusters & clouds that train frontier models.

But China can't read Washington's mind. It only sees what the U.S. is willing to pay for, and has to guess: is this real resolve… or just talk?
This is a signaling game. You'll play the U.S., send a costly signal, and watch China update its beliefs, sometimes in ways you didn't intend.

The math, if you want it
China updates with Bayes' rule:
μ(H|s) = Pr(s|H)·p ⁄ [ Pr(s|H)·p + Pr(s|L)·(1−p) ]
China adapts once belief clears its threshold:
μ(H|s) ≥ μ* = (q + l) ⁄ (g + d + l)
q = cost of adapting · g = gain if the U.S. is High · d = danger of waiting · l = option value of waiting.
The paradox: even a perfectly credible signal (μ→1) can make adapting the smart move, so credibility can accelerate the very substitution it tried to prevent.
