Can a policy be too credible?
The U.S. wants to slow China's AI. So it reaches for compute, the chips, clusters & clouds that train frontier models.

But China can't read Washington's mind. It only sees what the U.S. is willing to pay for, and has to guess: is this real resolve… or just talk?
This is a signaling game. You'll play the U.S., send a costly signal, and watch China update its beliefs, sometimes in ways you didn't intend.

The math, if you want it
China updates with Bayes' rule:
μ(H|s) = Pr(s|H) · pPr(s|H) · p + Pr(s|L) · (1−p)
p = China's prior that the U.S. is High Resolve
China adapts once belief clears its threshold:
μ(H|s) ≥ μ* = q + ℓg + d + ℓ
q = cost of adapting · g = gain if the U.S. is High · d = danger of waiting · ℓ = option value of waiting.
The paradox: even a perfectly credible signal (μ→1) can make adapting the smart move, so credibility can accelerate the very substitution it tried to prevent.

When compute policy becomes the signal

Costly signaling has already been used to study diplomacy, deterrence, reputation, cyber conflict, and institutions. But compute governance is still a missing case.

This game connects three pieces:

Fearon explains the credibility problem A signal matters when it is costly enough to separate resolve from bluffing.
Quek gives the cost map Signals can be sunk, tied-hands, installment, or reducible.
Compute governance gives the strategic site Chips, cloud access, thresholds, and provider oversight are visible enough to govern, and costly enough to signal.

So the game is not only about who controls compute. It is about how compute policies change beliefs, trigger adaptation, and reshape Vietnam's room to move.
